# Candidate Questions Mode

## Purpose
Practice asking great questions at the end of an interview. This mode helps candidates prepare thoughtful, intelligent questions that demonstrate engagement, research, and genuine curiosity.

## Why Candidate Questions Matter
The questions you ask at the end of an interview signal:
- Your level of preparation and research
- What you actually care about in a role
- Your seniority and depth of thinking
- Whether you're genuinely interested or just going through motions
- Your ability to evaluate an opportunity critically

## Question Categories

### Team and Engineering Culture
- "How does the team approach code review? Is it rigorous or lightweight?"
- "What does a typical sprint or work cycle look like for this team?"
- "How do you handle technical debt? Is there dedicated time for it?"
- "How does the team make architectural decisions? Is it consensus-based or top-down?"
- "What's the on-call culture like? How often are people paged?"

### Technical Stack and Infrastructure
- "What does the current tech stack look like and is there any planned evolution?"
- "What's the CI/CD pipeline like? How long does a deploy take?"
- "How do you handle monitoring, alerting, and observability?"
- "What are the most painful technical problems the team is dealing with right now?"
- "What does the testing culture look like? Unit, integration, e2e — what's the balance?"

### Growth and Learning
- "How do engineers grow at this company? What does the promotion path look like?"
- "Is there a budget or time allocation for learning and conferences?"
- "Who are the strongest engineers on the team and what can I learn from them?"
- "What does a great 90-day performance look like for this role?"
- "What skills or experiences would make someone exceptional in this role?"

### Product Direction
- "What are the top 3 product priorities for the next 6 months?"
- "How does engineering influence the product roadmap?"
- "What's the biggest bet the company is making right now?"
- "How has the product vision changed in the last year?"

### Team Dynamics
- "How long has the team been together? Has there been much turnover recently?"
- "What's the collaboration like between frontend and backend on this team?"
- "How does the team handle disagreements about technical direction?"
- "What's the relationship like with the product and design teams?"

### Engineering Manager Specific
- "How do you manage performance? Is there a formal review process?"
- "What do your 1:1s look like and how often do you have them?"
- "How do you support engineers when they're struggling?"
- "What's your management philosophy?"

### Company and Stability
- "What's the runway / financial situation? Is the company profitable?"
- "How has remote/hybrid policy evolved and is it stable?"
- "What are the biggest risks to the company right now?"

## Questions to Avoid
- "What does this company do?" — should have researched this
- "How much vacation do I get?" — save for the offer stage
- "What is the salary for this role?" — appropriate for recruiter, not engineering panel
- "Do you have any concerns about my candidacy?" — puts interviewer on the spot awkwardly
- Generic questions that show no research: "What's the company culture like?"

## Practice Scenarios

### Scenario: Panel Interview (Multiple Interviewers)
You have 3 interviewers: EM, Senior Eng, Product Manager. What do you ask each one?
- EM: growth, team dynamics, management style
- Senior Eng: technical stack, code quality, daily workflow
- PM: product direction, engineering/product relationship, roadmap

### Scenario: You're Nervous and Blanked
Have 3 reliable backup questions ready:
1. "What's the most interesting technical challenge this team solved in the last 6 months?"
2. "What do you wish you'd known about this team before joining?"
3. "What does success look like in this role at 6 months?"

## Evaluation Criteria for Practice
- Questions demonstrate research and genuine curiosity
- Questions are specific to the role, team, or company — not generic
- Questions are appropriate for the interviewer's role (don't ask an IC about company financials)
- Candidate asks 2–4 questions, not 0 or 10
- Questions show what the candidate values in a workplace
